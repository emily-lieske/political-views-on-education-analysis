Do Democratic and Republican Voters View Primary and Secondary Education Differently?

This project investigates whether Democratic and Republican voters hold statistically different views on primary and secondary education in the United States. As political climates have intensified public debate around school curricula, teaching standards, and institutional bias, understanding how party affiliation shapes attitudes toward foundational education has become increasingly relevant for policymakers and educators alike.

Using survey data, we conducted a rigorous statistical analysis designed to capture genuine sentiments about primary and secondary schooling across party lines — deliberately avoiding leading or politically charged framing to reduce response bias. Our methodology draws on hypothesis testing and exploratory data analysis to evaluate whether observed differences in attitudes are statistically significant or attributable to chance.

The findings are intended to provide actionable insights for policymakers navigating education reform, offering an empirically grounded perspective on public opinion rather than one shaped by partisan media narratives. Beyond policy, this research raises broader awareness about whether meaningful divides in education sentiment truly exist between the two parties — or whether perceived divisions are overstated.

Key components of this project include data cleaning and preparation, exploratory data analysis with visualizations, and formal statistical testing. The analysis was conducted in R and presented as a reproducible research report.

This work was completed as part of the Statistics for Data Science course (W203) at UC Berkeley's Master of Information and Data Science (MIDS) program.
